Case Study: Studify

Overview

Studify is a vocabulary-learning app that allows users to easily create and customize flashcards, as well as upload and attach media, to facilitate their study goals.

Goal: To design a digital flashcard app that optimizes efficient and effective vocabulary learning by maximizing study incentives and minimizing confusion and clutter.

Role: UX Researcher, UX Designer, UI Designer

Tools: Figma, Marvel, Draw.io, Pen/Paper

Research

Competitive Analysis

My first order of business for this project was to discover the vocabulary-learning app market. I chose three mobile apps – Quizlet, Flash Cards, and flashcards.io – to investigate existing flashcard-style study design solutions, compare strengths and weaknesses, and begin forming a picture of the ideal flashcard app.

Key Takeaways:

Quizlet undoubtedly has the most sleek and professional look of the three apps, which gives it great potential; however, the monetizing/advertising aspects of this app (i.e. account creation before revealing the app’s core features and constant upgrade pop-ups) is off-putting and drawn out enough to seriously hinder the user’s goals of creating flash cards quickly and studying efficiently.

Flash Cards, on the other hand, is the most bare-bones utilitarian of the three apps, which probably makes it the most quick to use, if not for the glitchy navigation and ad pop-ups.

flashcards.io is the most successful of the three competitor apps I have researched: its balance of appealing UI, intuitive interaction design, and extra helpful features best allow a stressed student to quickly create flash cards, organize cards into decks, and reinforce the memorization of new vocabulary.

User Interviews

Having delved into the flashcard app competitive landscape, I wanted to ask real people about their study habits, goals, and points of frustration when learning vocabulary. I was able to secure interviews with three participants aged 30-40, all currently studying vocabulary terms in preparation for upcoming exams in art therapy, energy code inspection, and import design, respectively.

Key Takeaways:

Most participants emphasized the importance of efficiency when studying vocabulary in the context of their busy, often stressful professional and personal lives. As such, having an intuitive flashcard app that can be easily used on-the-go is a high priority.

All participants remarked that they are best able to retain/memorize vocabulary via more than one learning method, i.e. visual associations, oral reinforcement, and/or physically writing down terms and definitions. An important challenge, then, is to design a digital flashcard app that can accommodate and integrate these multiple modalities.

Although most participants noted that the practical application of what they were studying served as a motivating factor, all of them expressed feelings of boredom while studying vocabulary. They also stated that making study sessions into games and/or challenges would greatly increase their incentive to study.

Ideation

Proto-persona: Orion Valdez

Based on the insights I gained from conducting user interviews, I created a proto-persona named Orion Valdez, who represents Studify’s primary target audience: young professionals studying for exams.

User Stories

Next, I distilled Orion’s behaviors, needs, and goals into the following four user stories:

As a busy freelance professional, I want a quick and intuitive way to make digital flashcards so that I can easily study for my upcoming graphic design online certification exam.

As a technology-ambivalent millennial, I want to be able to physically write terms and definitions down initially so that I can concretely commit terms to memory like I did as a student growing up.

As a visual learner, I want the option to categorize certain subgroups of terms using different colors, fonts, and images, so that I can form visual associations and thus better retain term definitions.

As someone easily bored when studying, I want to change up the digital flashcard format sometimes, like doing multiple choice mini-quizzes or daily challenges with one or more other users, so that I can vary the studying format and better motivate myself to reinforce my learning.

Problem and Hypothesis Statements

I then further summarized Orion’s chief pain points in the form of a problem statement, accompanied by three hypothesis statements, which propose ways Studify will attempt to solve Orion’s problems.

Orion needs a more efficient, customizable, and motivating way to study specialized vocabulary because they are a busy young professional balancing freelance gigs with taking an upcoming graphic design online certification exam. We know this to be true when we see that Orion can upload and personalize their own digital flashcard decks, as well as generate various game-based study challenges on their vocabulary learning app.

We believe that by developing a digital flashcard app that allows Orion to upload their own written definitions, and/or synthesize their own typed definitions via integrated search engine results, we will help Orion commit terminology to memory quickly and effectively.

We believe that by creating visually-appealing font and color choices, as well as allowing Orion to attach images/media to their digital flashcards, we will help Orion customize their decks and create helpful visual associations for holistic learning.

We believe that by implementing game-style study challenges, such as daily multiple-choice mini-quizzes or short competitions against fellow “study buddies” also using the app, we will help motivate Orion to take time out of their busy schedule to study.

Iteration

Wireframing

Having strategized Studify’s basic layout and components according to my hypothesis statements, I began sketching out the app’s actual screens, creating low-fidelity wireframes of the user flows I had just diagrammed.

User Testing

After completing an initial low-fidelity mobile prototype for Studify, I drew up a Test Plan and Test Script and conducted three moderated user tests, asking each participant three initial background questions and five short scenario tasks to complete using my prototype.

Key Takeaways:

Users were unsure whether to type “term” or “definition” first while creating a new flashcard. Recommendation: only allow “term” field entry at first, then automatically add “definition” field once term has been entered.

Users had trouble with saving their new flashcard, and receiving confirmation that the card was saved. Recommendation: enlarge “save” button and change icon to plus sign, include confirmation screen afterwards.

Users were confused navigating daily challenge settings. Recommendation: create a separate screen for daily challenge settings rather than a drop-down menu.

High-Fidelity Prototype

More recently, I picked this case study back up and decided to transform my previous wireframes into a high-fidelity mobile prototype using Figma. During this process, I ended up revising Studify’s features further, based on Google’s Material Design guidelines and other design tips I’d learned since completing the initial project. I also added basic brand colors and clarified Studify’s typography and iconography stylings.

Some of the main design improvements I made included:

  • Adding text labels to navigation icons for clarity/accessibility
  • Implementing a floating action button for certain screens, representing primary actions/CTAS
  • Adding a scan function to convert text from images of written flashcards into typed text that can be saved digitally
  • Confirming flashcard saves with fewer screens/in a less cumbersome way by using snackbar messaging
  • Developing flow for adding definitions via speech input

Lessons and Next Steps

Studify was my first experience following the design thinking process and learning the tenets of UX Design. I distilled the major insights I gained from this project into the following directives:

  • Simplify – represent each discrete action with a separate screen
  • Reduce – take all unnecessary clutter out of each screen, maximize space
  • Balance – negotiate between hierarchical and flat navigation, include backward flows
  • Confirm – take the time to include confirmation screens to assure user(s)
  • Suggest – don’t give away every step of the task, set up elements to suggest the next action
  • Assess – don’t simply adapt every single suggestion, weigh input against my own inclinations
